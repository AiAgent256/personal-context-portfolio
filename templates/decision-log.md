## Key Decision: Germany as EU Beachhead (vs Ireland, UK)

**Context:** Haven256 wants to expand custody services for government-seized digital assets into Europe. Recently attended ACAMS conference in Estonia as part of this push. Needed to pick a country to focus on first.

**Options evaluated:** Germany, Ireland, UK

**Framework applied — prioritized based on:**
1. Is there a large volume of crypto seizures? (demand signal)
2. Does the government work with 3rd-party contractors for services? (market accessibility)
3. What's the MiCA/regulatory timeline vs other areas? (speed to market)
4. Is the area amenable to selling other service lines? (cross-sell/expansion potential)
5. Does Patrick Holland (lead gov relations) have strong relationships with those agencies? (execution advantage)

**Why not UK:**
- FCA rules, not MiCA — different regulatory framework
- More competition, more contracts, but Haven256 is a late entrant
- Contracts take 1-2 years to win and last 5-8 years — late entry makes the math worse
- Being late + long sales cycles + long contract durations = potentially locked out for years

**Why Germany won:**
- Scored well across the prioritization criteria
- MiCA alignment (EU-wide framework, not UK-specific)
- Strong enough signals on seizure volume and contractor openness
- Patrick Holland's relationships factored in

**What this reveals about Andrew's decision-making:**
- Builds multi-factor frameworks for high-stakes decisions rather than going with gut alone
- Heavily weights derisking — with $750K+ in committed spend, wrong country choice is very costly
- Prioritizes speed to revenue but won't enter a market just because it's big if the competitive dynamics are unfavorable
- Relationship capital (Patrick's network) is treated as a real strategic asset, not a nice-to-have

## Pending Decision: Hedge Fund Proposal Timing & Approach

**Context:** Andrew wants to propose running a directional crypto hedge fund within the multi-family office. Strategy centers on the 4-year BTC cycle — spot BTC, leveraged positions, MicroStrategy equity (leverage beta to BTC), and possibly MicroStrategy LEAP calls.

**Key considerations being weighed:**
1. **Audience segmentation:** Todd (managing partner) is ex-commodity trader, gut decision-maker, doesn't understand or agree with BTC thesis, doesn't like to read, forms strong opinions fast in either direction. Once blocked, needs an external force (internal champion, podcast he trusts) to reopen. Other partners are analytically and data driven — respond well to PowerPoints, IC memos with specific points, walking through nuance. Bob trusts Andrew completely.
2. **Timing:** Partners are closing a mid-9-figure powered land deal this week. Proposing during deal close is bad timing. Other firm members are starting to make crypto-related asks — creates both urgency (stake a claim) and risk (gets lumped in with less strategic proposals).
3. **Ownership:** Firm is growing, people get excited, "too many cooks" slows progress. Andrew wants this 100% owned by him while allowing others to help. This would be his first truly solo enterprise at the firm.
4. **Format:** Two-layer approach planned:
   - **High-level PowerPoint** for first-pass acceptance — designed for both Todd and the partners as an initial read
   - **Full IC memo** covering: fund structure, legal and operating procedures, fundamental and technical thesis, Andrew's experience as a trader, risk profile, return expectations, duration, trade execution plan, exposure options (crypto/equities/derivatives), update cadence. Super technical — designed for the analytically driven partners.
5. **Todd psychology:** Andrew has never successfully pushed Todd into something Todd was skeptical on from the start. Todd's mind gets opened by external forces, not internal persuasion. This means the path to Todd's buy-in may be indirect — other partners opting in, external validation sources, or Bob as internal champion.
6. **Jason risk:** Jason (partner, engineer/BTC guy) will likely say it's a good idea but may claim he can do it himself — potential competing ownership claim.
7. **Kevin dynamic:** Newest partner, crypto-inclined, may agree to not stir anything — supportive but passive.
8. **Alternative paths:** Other partners can allocate funds independently — it's not a binary yes/no from Todd. Andrew can get capital even without Todd's full buy-in.
9. **Origin story leverage:** Todd originally hired Andrew after Andrew proved a 10x trade (longing Covid during the crypto bear market). The investing credibility is foundational to the relationship — this is a card to play.

**What this reveals about Andrew's decision-making:**
- Thinks carefully about stakeholder management — doesn't just build the best argument, thinks about who needs what format
- Timing and context matter as much as the substance of the proposal
- Protective of ownership and execution velocity — has learned that broad involvement can kill momentum
- Strategic about organizational positioning — aware of how other people's asks affect the landscape for his own
- Realistic about what he can and can't influence — recognizes when direct persuasion won't work and looks for alternative paths
- Pragmatic about alternative paths to yes — if Todd doesn't buy in, other partners can still allocate
